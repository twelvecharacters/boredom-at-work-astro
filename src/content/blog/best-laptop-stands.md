## Why Use a Laptop Stand?

If you're on the fence, here's what a laptop stand actually does for you:

### 1. Fixes Your Posture (and Neck Pain)

When your laptop sits flat on a desk, the screen is 4-6 inches below eye level. Your head tilts forward, your shoulders round, and your neck muscles work overtime to hold that position for 8 hours. A stand raises the screen to eye level, which keeps your spine neutral and your neck relaxed.

### 2. Brings the Screen to Eye Level

The top of your screen should be roughly at or slightly below your eye line. Most laptop stands raise the screen 5-7 inches, which puts a 13-15" laptop at the right height for most people. Taller users may want adjustable stands that go higher (like the Roost V3 at up to 12.5").

### 3. Improves Airflow and Cooling

Laptops generate heat. When they sit flat on a desk, the bottom vents get partially blocked. A stand lifts the laptop off the surface, allowing air to circulate underneath. Metal stands (especially aluminum) also act as passive heat sinks. Cooler laptops mean better performance and longer component life.

### 4. Frees Up Desk Space

A laptop on a stand takes up vertical space instead of just horizontal space. This opens up room underneath for an [external keyboard](/best-mechanical-keyboards-beginners/), notepads, or just breathing room on your desk.

## How to Choose the Right Laptop Stand

### Fixed vs. Adjustable

| Type | Pros | Cons | Best For |
|------|------|------|----------|
| **Fixed height** | Cleaner design, more stable, usually cheaper | Can't customize height | Permanent desk setups, one user |
| **Adjustable** | Dial in perfect height, works for multiple users | More moving parts, can wobble | Shared desks, sit-to-stand setups |

**Rule of thumb:** If you have a dedicated desk and know your ideal screen height, go fixed. If you share a workspace, switch between sitting and standing, or aren't sure what height you need, go adjustable.

### Material Matters

| Material | Cooling | Durability | Weight | Price |
|----------|---------|------------|--------|-------|
| **Aluminum** | Excellent (heat sink) | High | Medium | $$-$$$ |
| **Metal mesh** | Good (airflow) | Medium | Light | $ |
| **Glass-filled nylon** | None | High | Very light | $$$ |
| **Plastic** | Poor (insulates heat) | Low-Medium | Light | $ |

Aluminum is the sweet spot for most desk stands. For travel, glass-filled nylon (like the Roost V3) wins on weight.

### Portability

If you travel or switch workspaces, weight and folded size matter:

| Stand | Folded? | Weight | Portability Rating |
|-------|---------|--------|--------------------|
| Roost V3 | Yes, smartphone-sized | 6 oz | Excellent |
| UGREEN Foldable | Yes, compact | 0.52 lbs | Very good |
| Nulaxy C1 | Yes | ~1.5 lbs | Good |
| Rain Design mStand | No | 2.89 lbs | Poor |

### Don't Forget the Keyboard

Here's the thing everyone forgets: a laptop stand raises your screen **and** your keyboard. Once the screen is at eye level, the built-in keyboard is at an awkward angle. You need an [external keyboard](/best-mechanical-keyboards-beginners/) and mouse to actually get the ergonomic benefit.

Budget an extra ~$30-80 for a decent external keyboard if you don't already have one. A [good ergonomic mouse](/best-ergonomic-mice/) helps too.
