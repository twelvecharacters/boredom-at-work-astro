YouTube is the #1 platform for learning about investing — 65% of Americans who use social media for financial info prefer it over any other platform. But the quality ranges from genuinely life-changing to dangerously misleading.

Here are the channels actually worth your time, organized by what you need, plus the red flags that should make you close the tab immediately.

## Best for Beginners

### 1. The Plain Bagel (Richard Coffin)

**Subscribers:** ~1M+ | **Focus:** Demystifying finance

The best finance educator on YouTube, period. Richard is a CFA charterholder who explains complex concepts without jargon, hype, or trying to sell you anything. His videos on index funds, market crashes, and "how does X actually work" are perfectly paced for beginners.

**Start with:** "Stock Market for Beginners" or "Index Funds vs. Mutual Funds"

### 2. Graham Stephan

**Subscribers:** ~5.15M | **Focus:** Personal finance, real estate, credit

Huge audience for a reason — Graham makes financial concepts entertaining without being clickbaity. Strong on credit scores, saving strategies, and income growth. Less focused on stock picking, more on building wealth through habits.

**Start with:** "How I Budget My Millions" or his credit score breakdown videos

### 3. The Money Guy Show (Brian Preston)

**Subscribers:** ~540K | **Focus:** Retirement planning, tax strategy

Hosted by a CPA/CFP/CFA — actual credentials behind the advice. Focuses on long-term wealth building, retirement planning, and tax optimization. Less exciting than some channels, but the advice is solid and conservative in the best way.

**Start with:** "Financial Order of Operations" (their signature framework)

### 4. Andrei Jikh

**Subscribers:** ~2.48M | **Focus:** Financial minimalism, investing basics

Known for his card-trick intros and clean editing. Andrei covers ETFs, index funds, and personal finance with an approachable style. Good for millennials who want straightforward investing advice.

**Start with:** "How I Invest My Money" or his dividend portfolio videos

## Best for Intermediate Investors

### 5. Ben Felix (PWL Capital)

**Subscribers:** ~1M+ | **Focus:** Evidence-based investing

If you want research-backed investment education, Ben Felix is unmatched. Every video cites academic papers and data. He's the antidote to "trust me bro" finance content. Heavy on factor investing, asset allocation, and why most active strategies underperform.

**Start with:** "The Problem with Index Funds" or "Why Jack Bogle Was Wrong"

### 6. Sven Carlin, Ph.D.

**Subscribers:** ~300K+ | **Focus:** Value investing, stock analysis

Academic rigor applied to individual stock picks. Sven does deep fundamental analysis on specific companies, showing his entire thought process. Great for learning how to evaluate businesses, not just stock prices.

**Start with:** Any of his "Stock Analysis" deep-dive videos

### 7. The Swedish Investor

**Subscribers:** ~500K+ | **Focus:** Value investing, book summaries

Animated summaries of investing classics — Buffett, Graham, Lynch, Dalio. Perfect for getting the key insights from a 300-page investing book in 15 minutes. Then read the actual book if the ideas resonate.

**Start with:** "The Intelligent Investor" or "One Up on Wall Street" summaries

## Channels to Avoid (Red Flags)

I'm not naming specific channels because they come and go. Instead, here are the patterns that should make you skeptical:

### 1. "This Stock Will 10x!" (Pump and Dump)

If someone promotes a specific small-cap stock with an extreme price target and urgency ("buy before Friday!"), they likely own it and want you to push the price up so they can sell. This is literally illegal, but enforcement is slow.

### 2. Luxury Lifestyle as "Proof"

Lamborghinis, mansions, and first-class flights in the thumbnail. Real wealth builders rarely flex — and the people flexing are usually making money from courses, not investing.

### 3. "Banks Don't Want You to Know This"

Conspiracy-flavored financial advice is almost always wrong or misleading. Banks are not hiding secret wealth strategies. If it sounds too good to be true, it is.

### 4. No Credentials, No Transparency

Check: do they disclose their holdings? Do they have any financial credentials (CFA, CFP, CPA)? Do they say "this is not financial advice" and actually mean it? If the answer is no to all three, proceed with extreme caution.

### 5. Pivoted From Unrelated Content

A lifestyle or gaming YouTuber who suddenly becomes a finance expert during a bull market is usually chasing views, not sharing knowledge. Finance requires domain expertise — being entertaining doesn't qualify.

## How to Use Finance YouTube Effectively

**Do:**
- Watch educational content about *principles* (how to evaluate a stock, what an ETF is, how compound interest works)
- Cross-reference claims with other sources
- Use it as a starting point, not an endpoint
- Follow creators with credentials and transparent portfolios

**Don't:**
- Buy or sell based on a single video
- Follow stock picks without doing your own research
- Subscribe to paid communities or "signal groups"
- Assume high subscriber count = good advice
