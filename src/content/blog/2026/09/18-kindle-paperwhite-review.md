If you want a single recommendation and nothing else, here it is: the Kindle Paperwhite is the e-reader most people should buy in 2026. It has been our default pick across the site for a while, and the current model does nothing to change that.

But "most people" is not everyone, and $159.99 is real money. So here is an honest look at what the Paperwhite gets right, where it falls short, and who should spend more or less instead. (If you have not settled on Kindle at all yet, start with [which Kindle you should buy](/which-kindle-should-you-buy/) or our [Kindle vs Kobo](/kindle-vs-kobo/) comparison.)

## The Verdict Up Front

**Rating: 4.5 out of 5.** The Paperwhite is the complete package for reading: big enough screen, waterproof, comfortable in any lighting, and a battery you charge every few weeks. It loses half a point for things that are true of nearly every Kindle: you are locked into Amazon's store and formats, and there are no physical page-turn buttons.

**Buy it if** you want the best all-around e-reader without overthinking it. **Look elsewhere if** you need color, note-taking, or buttons.

## What You Get for $159.99

The current Paperwhite covers the fundamentals that actually matter day to day:

- **7-inch, 300 ppi display.** Text is crisp and print-like, and the larger screen means fewer page turns than the 6-inch base Kindle.
- **Faster page turns.** Amazon rebuilt this generation to be noticeably quicker than older Paperwhites, which matters more than it sounds when you are flipping pages for an hour.
- **IPX8 waterproofing.** Read in the bath or at the pool without worrying.
- **Adjustable warm light.** Shift the screen from cool white to a warm amber for comfortable night reading. This is the single biggest comfort upgrade over the base model.
- **Weeks of battery.** Amazon rates it at up to 12 weeks. Real-world use depends on brightness and how much you read, but you will charge it rarely.
- **16GB storage and USB-C.** Enough for thousands of books, with a modern charging port.

None of these are flashy, and that is the point. The Paperwhite is not trying to impress you with features. It just removes every small annoyance that would otherwise interrupt your reading.

## The Reading Experience

This is where the Paperwhite earns its reputation. The screen is glare-free in direct sunlight, the warm light makes late-night reading easy on the eyes, and the extra inch of screen over the base Kindle genuinely reduces how often you tap to turn the page.

For anyone reading mostly novels and nonfiction, this is as good as black-and-white e-reading gets. Pair it with [Kindle Unlimited](/kindle-unlimited-worth-it/) if you read a lot, or [sideload your own books](/sideload-books-kindle/) if you bring content from elsewhere, and it handles both without fuss.

## Paperwhite vs Signature Edition

For $40 more, the **Paperwhite Signature Edition** ($199.99) adds three things: 32GB of storage, an auto-adjusting front light that reacts to the room, and wireless charging. The screen, size, and reading experience are identical.

Our take: skip it unless you specifically want wireless charging. 16GB already holds far more books than most people will ever load, and the manual brightness slider is not exactly a hardship. The $40 is better spent on a good [Kindle case](/best-kindle-cases-2026/).

## Where It Falls Short

An honest review has to cover the downsides, and the Paperwhite has a few:

- **You are locked into Amazon.** No native EPUB support, and purchased books carry Amazon DRM. If you value format freedom or borrow heavily from libraries, [Kobo](/kindle-paperwhite-vs-kobo-clara/) is worth a serious look.
- **No physical page buttons.** With the Oasis discontinued, no current Kindle has them. Kobo still does on some models.
- **No color.** If you read comics, magazines, or illustrated books, the Paperwhite's black-and-white screen is a real limitation. That is what the [Colorsoft](/kindle-colorsoft-vs-paperwhite/) is for.
- **Ads by default.** The standard config shows sponsored screensavers unless you pay a small one-time fee to remove them.

## Who Should Buy Something Else

- **Want color?** Step up to the Kindle Colorsoft (from $249.99).
- **Want to take notes or annotate?** Get the [Kindle Scribe](/kindle-scribe-review/) (from $429.99).
- **Want physical buttons or EPUB freedom?** Consider a Kobo instead.
- **On a strict budget?** The base Kindle ($109.99) reads the same books, just without the warm light and waterproofing.

## Is It Worth It?

Yes. The Kindle Paperwhite is the e-reader we recommend to the most people, because it gets the fundamentals right and skips the compromises of the cheaper model without pushing into premium prices. At $159.99 it is the safe, sensible choice, and the one you are least likely to regret.
